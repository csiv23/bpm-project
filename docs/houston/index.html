Gold Star Records

Although Houston is a diverse cultural mosaic today, it wasn’t always like that. But now, when people spend time in Houston, they forget they’re in the South! This is because Houston has grown to appreciate and love what it has: peoples and cultures. It wasn’t easy using these resources to become the beautiful cohort we are today though, people had to endure hardship for where we are today. Many of these people were Black people who were struggling financially. Gold Star Recording Studios changed that though, Gold Star Recording Studios enabled people who knew they had the artist in them and encouraged them to explore that artist. By extension, this helped a lot of people out of unhealthy financial situations they were locked in due to the economic inequality that plagues all communities of the U.S. Some of these individuals include Brian McKnight, Carl Mitchell, Solange Knowles, Kelly Rowland, Michelle Williams, Hubert Laws, and Beyonce.

Clifton Chenier

Before diving into the man known as King of the Bayou, King of the South, and King of Zydeco, I’d like to highlight some of his awards as a result of the strides he made in proving that Black Popular Music can fight economic inequality. Several of these awards include the National Heritage Fellowship awarded by the National Endowment for the Arts, a place in the Blues Hall of Fame, an album considered by the Library of Congress to be "culturally, historically, or artistically significant" and selected it for preservation in the National Recording Registry. Clifton Chenier was most famous for playing his folksy accordion. He traveled all over the nation, but, he really made a difference down in Texas. Clifton Chenier made his way to Texas and played in different blues clubs, trying to get more people involved to grow both his wealth, and the blues. Chenier’s contributions to fighting against economic inequality live on in the spirit that drives successful blues clubs today. Chenier paved a road that allowed blue and folk musicians to explore their art and not have to worry about finances.

Robert Earl Davis Jr.

Rober Davis Jr. or better known by his stage name, DJ Screw, was a writer, rapper, producer, and visionary who explored his art and defied society in Houston. DJ Screw began his career when he was just 12 years old where he first discovered his slowed down mixes. After a few friends bought some his mixtapes, several community memebers were in awe and began requesting his tapes. Once Robert had a little money and a gig going, he began inviting some of Houston’s MCs that lived their lives on the south side of Houston to rap and/or freestyle on some of his mixes. This lead to the founding of Houston’s Screwed Up Clicked which included several musicians who became prominent figures in the community. DJ Screw’s music and passion for bringing people together enabled these individuals to come together and pave a new path for new Black Music. By extension, DJ Screw paved a path for those who are economically disadvantaged to explore themselves and their art within and capitalize money and community off of it.
